How to Optimize Your Google Business Profile (Step-by-Step for 2026)

July 2026 · 7 min read · TakeHold Media

Most Google Business Profiles are set up once and forgotten. Name, address, phone number — maybe a few photos from years ago — and that's it. Then business owners wonder why they're not showing up in local search.

A default GBP is not the same thing as an optimized one. The gap between the two is significant — and closing it doesn't require any technical expertise. It just requires knowing what Google actually looks at.

Here's a section-by-section walkthrough of what fully optimized looks like.

Start Here: Claim and Verify Your Listing

Before you can optimize anything, you have to own it. Go to business.google.com and search for your business. If a listing already exists — and Google may have created one automatically — claim it. If there's nothing there, create one from scratch.

Verification typically happens via postcard (Google mails a code to your business address), phone, or email. This step is non-negotiable. An unclaimed listing can have wrong information, missing photos, and zero ability for you to respond to reviews. Competitors can sometimes even suggest edits that change your details.

What fully optimized looks like: Claimed, verified, and showing a green checkmark in your GBP dashboard. If you're managing multiple locations, each one should be claimed individually.

Business Name, Category, and Description

Your business name on GBP should match your real-world name exactly. Not "Orlando Best Plumber" — just "Smith Plumbing." Google penalizes keyword-stuffing in business names, and it looks unprofessional to anyone who sees it.

Primary category is one of the most important ranking signals in your entire profile. It tells Google what type of business you are, which determines which searches you're eligible to appear in. Choose the most specific option available. "Digital Marketing Agency" beats "Marketing Agency." "Dentist" beats "Health Care Provider."

You can also add secondary categories — these are useful when you offer distinct services. A company that does both web design and SEO might list both as categories.

Your business description gets 750 characters. Use them. Write naturally, mention your primary services, mention your city, and explain what makes your business different. Don't pack it with keywords — write for the person reading it, and the ranking benefit will follow.

What default looks like: Business name entered once, primary category set to something broad, description left blank or copied from the website's "About" page with no local signals.

Address, Service Area, and Hours

If customers come to you, your address should be visible. If you go to customers — or you operate from a home address you don't want public — you can hide the address and instead define a service area by city, county, or radius.

Service area matters for rankings. If you serve all of Central Florida, list it. Google uses service area data to decide whether to show you for searches in those locations.

Hours need to be accurate and kept current. Google shows when a business is open right in search results. A business listed as "closed" during business hours loses credibility instantly. Update your hours for holidays, and use the "More hours" feature if your phone hours differ from your walk-in hours.

Common mistake: Leaving hours unset, or setting them once and never updating them for holidays. Google will sometimes pull incorrect hours from other sources if yours aren't kept current.

Phone Number and Website

Use your primary business phone number — the same one that appears on your website and in any directories. Consistency across every platform (what's known as NAP consistency: Name, Address, Phone) is a ranking signal. If your GBP says one number and Yelp says another, Google notices the discrepancy.

Link to your actual website homepage, not a landing page or a scheduling tool. If you have a scheduling link you want people to use, that's what the "Appointment links" field is for.

Services and Products

This section is underused by most businesses. GBP lets you list your specific services with names, descriptions, and prices (if applicable). Each service you list gives Google another signal about what you do — which means more searches you're potentially eligible to show up in.

Be specific. Instead of just "SEO," list "Local SEO," "Google Business Profile Optimization," and "Citation Building" as separate entries. Instead of just "Lawn Care," list "Lawn Mowing," "Fertilization," and "Sod Installation." The more specific you are, the more precisely Google can match you to the right searches.

What fully optimized looks like: Every core service listed individually with a 2–3 sentence description. Products listed with photos if relevant. Prices included where they help set expectations.

Photos and Videos

Businesses with photos get significantly more clicks and direction requests than businesses without them. This is not a small difference — Google's own data has consistently shown this for years.

What to upload: your storefront or office exterior (so people know what to look for), your team at work, examples of your work or products, and your logo. Videos should be under 30 seconds and show something real — a project walkthrough, a satisfied client, your process.

Avoid stock photos. Google can detect them, and customers definitely can. A real photo of your actual work beats a generic stock image every time.

Keep adding photos over time. Recency matters. A profile with 40 photos added three years ago performs worse than one with 20 photos added consistently over the past year.

Reviews: The Most Powerful Section You Can't Fully Control

Reviews influence your local ranking more than almost any other factor. The number of reviews, how recent they are, and your average rating all feed into where Google places you in the Local Pack.

You can't write your own reviews — but you can systematically ask for them. Send every satisfied customer a direct link to your Google review page. Make it one click. Ask within 24–48 hours of a completed job, when the experience is fresh.

Respond to every review. Thank the positive ones by name if possible. Address the negative ones calmly and professionally — your response is as much for the next potential customer reading it as it is for the person who left the review.

Aim for: A minimum of 10 reviews before you start running ads or making other marketing investments. Without reviews, traffic you drive to your profile has nothing to land on. At 25+ reviews with a 4.5+ average, you've built a foundation that does work for you passively.

Google Posts: The Feature Almost Nobody Uses

GBP lets you publish posts directly on your profile — offers, updates, events, and announcements. These appear in search results when someone looks up your business. They expire after seven days (for most types), which means they require consistent upkeep.

Most businesses never touch this feature. That's an opportunity. A business that posts weekly signals to Google that it's active — and to customers that it's on top of things. Posts don't have a massive impact on rankings, but they contribute to overall profile completeness and engagement.

Keep posts short. One main point, a photo, and a link to your contact page or a relevant service page. Don't overthink it.

Q&A: Answer Before Someone Else Does

The Q&A section lets anyone ask — and answer — questions about your business. That includes you, but it also includes random people on the internet.

Check this section regularly. If common questions aren't answered, add them yourself. Think about what your customers actually ask before hiring you, and answer those questions preemptively. This makes your profile more useful and reduces the chance someone answers a question about your business incorrectly.

A Completed Profile vs. a Maintained One

There's a difference between a profile you fill out once and a profile you actively maintain. Google rewards businesses that stay current — new photos, new posts, fresh reviews, updated hours. A profile that's fully optimized today but untouched for a year will lose ground to a competitor who stays active.

  1. Claim and verify your listing at business.google.com — this is the prerequisite for everything else.
  2. Set your primary category as specifically as possible, then add relevant secondary categories.
  3. Complete every field — description, services, hours, website, phone — with accurate, consistent information.
  4. Upload at least 10 real photos of your business, team, and work. Add more every month.
  5. Ask every satisfied customer for a review and respond to every one you receive.
  6. Post an update once a week — offers, news, or a quick project highlight.
  7. Seed the Q&A section with the questions your customers actually ask.

This isn't a one-time project. It's a system. The businesses that dominate local search aren't doing anything exotic — they're doing these basics consistently, over time, while their competitors let their profiles go stale.
